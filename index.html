Battle-tested negotiation frameworks

Harvard method
Principled negotiation (Fisher & Ury)
The foundation of modern negotiation. Separate the people from the problem. Focus on interests, not positions. The goal is mutual gain, not victory over the other side.
1
Separate people from problem. Attack the issue, never the person. "The invoice timeline isn't working for either of us" not "You're being unreasonable."
2
Focus on interests, not positions. Ask why they want what they want. A client who says "₹2k/mo max" actually wants low financial risk — that's an interest you can address differently.
3
Invent options for mutual gain. Generate multiple possibilities before settling on one. "What if we did a 3-month trial at a lower rate with a commitment clause?"
4
Insist on objective criteria. Anchor to market data, standards, or precedent — not emotion. "Industry standard for this software tier is ₹4,000–₹7,000/mo. I'm proposing ₹5,000."
FBI method
The Voss framework (Chris Voss — Never Split the Difference)
Built for high-stakes hostage negotiation. Adapted for business. Focuses on emotional intelligence, not logic. People make decisions emotionally and justify rationally.
1
Tactical empathy. Identify and verbalize their emotional state. "It sounds like you've been burned by software vendors before." Makes them feel understood before you've agreed to anything.
2
Mirroring. Repeat the last 1–3 words they said as a question. They said: "We need a solution by next quarter." You say: "By next quarter?" Silence. They fill it with more information.
3
Labeling. "It seems like…" / "It sounds like…" / "It looks like…" Label emotions before addressing the issue. Diffuses tension without conceding anything.
4
Calibrated questions. Never "yes/no" questions. "How am I supposed to do that?" / "What would need to happen for this to work?" Forces them to solve your problem for you.
5
The "that's right" moment. When they say "that's right" (not "you're right"), you've achieved genuine understanding. That's when real movement begins.
Sales & SaaS context
The SPIN framework (Rackham)
Designed for complex B2B sales negotiations. Works perfectly for Apex CRM client conversations. Leads the other person to your solution through their own words.
S
Situation questions. Establish context. "How many people manage your invoicing currently?" "What tools do you use today?"
P
Problem questions. Surface the pain. "What's the most frustrating part of that process?" "How often do invoices get delayed?"
I
Implication questions. Amplify the cost of the problem. "When invoices are delayed, how does that affect your cash flow?" "What does your accountant spend time on because of this?"
N
Need-payoff questions. Let them articulate your solution. "If you could cut that reconciliation time by 70%, how much would that mean for your team?" They sell themselves.

Power tactics — how to use them and how to counter them

The flinch
both›
When the other side states a number, visibly react — a sharp exhale, a pause, "Hmm…" — before saying anything. This creates doubt in their mind about whether their number was too aggressive, and often causes them to soften their position before you've said a word.
How to use it
Client says "₹2,000/mo maximum." You pause, exhale slightly, then say nothing for 3 seconds. Let the silence sit. Then: "That's quite a bit below where we'd need to be to make this work…" Don't fill the gap.
How to counter it
Recognize the flinch for what it is — theatre. Stay calm. Don't reduce your number preemptively. Say: "I understand it seems high. Let me show you why the value is there." Then demonstrate, don't discount.
The anchor
offensive›
The first number stated in any negotiation becomes the psychological reference point for everything that follows. Research shows all subsequent numbers gravitate toward the anchor, even when people try to rationally ignore it.
How to use it
Always name a number first. Go higher than your target — not absurdly high (kills credibility), but 20–40% above where you'd be happy to land. For Apex pricing: if you want ₹5,000/mo, open with ₹8,000/mo. The final ₹5,500 feels like a win for them.
How to counter it
Don't negotiate from their anchor. Explicitly reject it: "That number doesn't work as a starting point. Here's what the market looks like, and here's where we need to be." Re-anchor with your number before any discussion of movement.
Good cop / bad cop
both›
One person is warm and agreeable ("I really like this proposal"), the other is skeptical or demanding ("My partner thinks this is way overpriced"). The "good cop" creates rapport; the "bad cop" creates pressure. Together they move you toward concessions you wouldn't otherwise make.
How to use it (solo version)
As a solo founder, use an imaginary "partner" or "board." "I'd love to do ₹3,000/mo, but my co-founder reviews all contracts and he won't approve anything below ₹5,000." Lets you be the good cop while an unnamed force holds the line.
How to counter it
Name it out loud: "It sounds like you two have different views on this — which of you has final authority to make a decision?" Forces the real decision-maker to step forward and collapses the dynamic.
Deadline pressure
offensive›
Artificial or real deadlines force decisions. People make concessions faster when time is running out. The key is making the deadline feel real and the consequence feel tangible.
How to use it
"This pricing is valid through Friday — after that, our next onboarding batch starts and pricing will reflect the new rate." Or: "I have two other companies interested in this slot. I can hold it until Thursday." Real or structural — it creates urgency without desperation.
How to counter it
Test the deadline. "What happens if we don't close by Friday?" If the answer is vague, it's artificial. Say: "I'd rather take a few more days and make the right decision than rush. Can we extend to next Wednesday?" Usually they say yes — proving the deadline was soft.
The nibble
offensive›
After the deal is essentially agreed, ask for one small additional concession. The other party has emotionally committed to the deal and is in a "yes" state — they're highly unlikely to blow it up over a small add-on. This is how experienced negotiators extract the last bit of value.
How to use it
After a client verbally agrees to ₹5,000/mo: "Great — and can we include onboarding setup and 1 month of data migration support in that?" They're already in yes-mode. The psychological cost of saying no to something small after agreeing to something big is high.
How to counter it
"I'm happy to include that, but we'd need to revisit the base price. Nothing changes without something changing." Makes them choose: keep the price, or reopen the entire deal. They almost always drop the nibble.
Strategic silence
defensive›
Most people are deeply uncomfortable with silence and will fill it — often with concessions, justifications, or information they didn't plan to reveal. The negotiator who can sit comfortably in silence has a massive structural advantage.
How to use it
After you state your price or make a demand — stop talking. Completely. Even if it's 8, 10, 15 seconds of silence. The other person will almost always speak first, and what they say will tell you everything about where they really are.
How to counter it
Recognize silence as a tactic. Ask a question to break it on your terms: "What are your thoughts?" — you're still responding but you're not conceding. Never reduce a number just because silence followed it.
The higher authority
defensive›
Claiming you need to check with someone else before committing. This gives you a genuine out, prevents in-the-moment pressure decisions, and lets you return with a harder position without personal conflict. Even if you ARE the final authority, using this tactic protects your position.
How to use it
"I'm very interested in this — let me run it by my business partner/advisor and I'll come back to you by tomorrow." Buys time to think, research alternatives, and return with a firmer position. Cannot be pressured to decide on the spot.
How to counter it
Before the negotiation begins, ask: "Are you the person who can make the final call today?" If yes, hold them to it. If they later invoke a higher authority, say: "I thought you had authority on this — what changed?"

Word-for-word scripts for real situations

Pricing pushback — "It's too expensive"
Works for Apex CRM, StockLens, any paid product
›
Them"This is more than we budgeted. Can you come down on price?"
You"I appreciate you being direct. Help me understand — is it that the overall number is too high, or is it more about cash flow and what hits the account each month?"
NoteThis separates two very different problems. A cash-flow issue → trial period, deferred start, or quarterly payment. A value issue → you need to build more case, not reduce price.
Them"Honestly it's both — ₹5,000 is just a lot per month."
You"I understand. Let me put it differently. You told me earlier your billing process wastes about 5 hours a week. At even a conservative ₹300/hour for your staff's time, that's ₹6,000 a month in waste. Apex costs ₹5,000. You're not spending money — you're recovering it."
NoteUse THEIR numbers from the discovery call. Never invent the ROI — they gave it to you. This is why the discovery call is non-negotiable.
You"What I can do is give you the first 30 days free. You run your real operations through it, no risk. If it doesn't deliver that value — walk away. Fair?"
The stall — "Let me think about it"
Most common deal-killer. Handle it immediately.
›
Them"This looks good. Let me think about it and I'll get back to you."
You"Absolutely. Just so I can follow up usefully — what specifically do you need to think through? Is it the pricing, the features, or something else?"
Note"Let me think about it" almost always means there's an unspoken objection. This question surfaces it. Whatever they say next is the real objection — handle that, not the stall.
Them"Mainly I need to check with my partner."
You"That makes complete sense. Would it be helpful if I joined a quick 15-minute call with both of you? I find it saves time compared to playing phone tag, and I can answer both your questions directly."
NoteAlways try to get into the room with the real decision maker. A second call with the partner present is 3x more likely to close than an email chain.
Competitor comparison — "X is cheaper"
Zoho, Tally, free tools — you'll hear this constantly
›
Them"Zoho CRM does something similar for ₹1,500 a month."
You"Zoho is a great product — for large teams with dedicated IT staff to configure it. Most of my clients tried Zoho first. What usually happened is they spent 2–3 months setting it up, it still didn't match their workflow, and they ended up back in Excel anyway."
NoteNever trash the competitor. Acknowledge strength, then reframe the context. "Great for X, not built for Y" is far more credible than "they're worse."
You"Apex is built specifically for Indian SMBs — GST invoicing, HRMS for Indian compliance, supplier tracking. Not a global tool adapted for India. The ₹3,500 difference is the cost of not spending 3 months on implementation. Plus you have my direct number, not a ticketing system."
Asking for a raise / better rate (personal)
Applies to salary, freelance rate, partnership terms
›
SetupResearch the market rate first. Know your number. Have a BATNA (another offer, another client, the ability to walk away). Without these, you're negotiating from weakness.
You"I've been tracking my output this past 6 months and I think there's a conversation worth having about my rate. Based on current market rates for what I deliver, I should be at ₹X. I'd like to get there."
NoteState the number. Don't ask "what do you think about a raise?" — that invites a counter before you've even anchored. State the number, then stop talking.
Them"That's a big jump. We can do ₹Y, which is more modest."
You"I appreciate that. ₹Y gets us closer, but based on the market data I've looked at and the scope I'm currently covering, I need to stay at ₹X to make this work long-term. What would need to be true for us to get there?"
Note"What would need to be true…" is a calibrated question that makes them solve your problem. You're not begging — you're problem-solving together.

The 10 immutable laws of negotiation

1
Everything is negotiable
Price, timeline, scope, payment terms, onboarding support, contract length, add-ons. If someone set the terms, someone can change them. The person who believes the terms are fixed loses before the conversation starts.
Apex application: "Standard price is ₹5,000/mo" is your opening — not your floor.
2
Preparation beats talent
The negotiator who knows their numbers, knows the other side's alternatives, and has walked through scenarios in advance will consistently beat the "naturally gifted" talker who wings it. Spend 3 hours preparing for a 30-minute negotiation.
Apex application: Know the prospect's industry before the discovery call. Know what Zoho and Tally charge. Know your cost floor.
3
Whoever cares less wins
Emotional detachment is a structural advantage. The moment the other side senses you need this deal, they have leverage. Build your BATNA specifically so you can walk away and genuinely mean it. The walk-away is the weapon; use it rarely but always have it loaded.
4
Never give without getting
Every concession must be traded, never gifted. "I can do ₹4,000 if we lock in a 12-month contract" is a trade. "I can do ₹4,000" is a gift that destroys your anchoring position and signals you were padding in the first place.
Apex application: Lower price = annual commitment, or reduced scope, or delayed start date. Never just lower the number.
5
The person who names a number first sets the range
The anchor effect is one of the most robust findings in behavioral psychology. First movers in price negotiations consistently get better outcomes. Waiting for the other side to name a number first only helps if they anchor low — and experienced negotiators won't.
6
Questions are more powerful than statements
"How are you supposed to do that at ₹2,000?" is more powerful than "₹2,000 doesn't work." Questions force the other side into your frame. Statements invite counterstatements. Questions invite thinking — and thinking people often talk themselves into your position.
7
Liking is leverage
People give better deals to people they like. This isn't manipulation — it's reality. Invest 5 minutes in genuine rapport before every negotiation. Find something real to connect on. A person who sees you as a human being negotiates differently than one who sees you as a vendor number.
8
Written beats verbal
Verbal agreements dissolve. Write down every agreement in real time — even a WhatsApp message summary after the call counts. "Just to confirm what we agreed: ₹4,500/mo starting June 1, 12-month term, data migration included." This prevents revisiting settled points and creates accountability.
9
Patience is a superpower
Deadlines move deals — usually toward the impatient party. If you can outlast the other side's urgency, the deal moves in your direction. Never rush a negotiation you're winning. Never panic in one you're losing.
10
The best deal is one they want to honor
A deal won through pressure, manipulation, or information asymmetry will be poorly executed, resented, or walked back. The best negotiators leave the other side feeling good about the outcome. That's not idealism — it's retention, referrals, and reputation.
Apex application: A client who felt heard and fairly treated becomes your best case study and referral source.
